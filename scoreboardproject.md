### Description of final project choice, why chosen, and what successful project would do

The project that I will be doing is a wooden scoreboard that displays the scores from NFL games, specifically the Carolina Panthers games. The project will automatically update the scores based on the game live. So, for example, if the Panthers score a touchdown, the score will then be displayed on the screens on the board. This is achievable by using the ESPN public API, and creating a code that will search for games and scores and retrieve them.

### Discussion of mistakes, issues, challenges, and how you overcome them

I encountered a few challenges during this project. The first issue was that I underestimated how long this would take, and it required me to come in a lot after school to work on some of the things. I also changed my plan on this project a few times. Initially, I had not planned to stain the wood, but I thought in the end it would make everything look better. I also did not plan to use the Polycrylic coating on the wood, but after figuring out its purpose and the benefit of using it, I decided to put 2 layers of it on the board. The biggest issue I had was with the screens. I had ordered 2 I2C screens because they only required 4 pins and were the right size for my board. However, I found out that for my specific project, there were not enough libraries to use, and the entire coding piece of this would be very difficult. I tried to make these work for a few days, but ultimately knew it would be better to find different screens since it was so close to the due date of the project. I ended up switching to the ili9341 screens, which were much easier to work with.

## Final Summary

This project taught me a lot about how much planning, testing, and revision goes into making something that looks simple in the end. When I first started, my ideas were much less developed, and I was still deciding between completely different directions for the project. Over time, I moved from smaller woodworking projects into a much larger idea that combined woodworking, design, electronics, and coding. I learned that a project like this is not just about building the final object. It is also about research, figuring out materials, testing components, and making decisions when something does not go the way you expected. Through this process, I learned more about Raspberry Pi systems, live data through APIs, screen compatibility, laser cutting, sanding, staining, and finishing wood. I also learned how much detail matters when trying to combine digital and physical parts into one working project.

One of the biggest things I learned about completing a large project is that flexibility matters just as much as planning. At the beginning, I thought my project would focus on Charlotte Latin sports, but after learning that live score updating would not really be possible in the way I wanted, I had to shift directions and focus on NFL games instead. I also changed the design itself. Originally, I planned to engrave the logo on a flat board, but later realized that a multilayered stadium design would be much more creative and visually interesting. That change made the project stronger, but it also made the process longer and more difficult. I had to spend extra time creating the stadium layer files, laser cutting each layer, fixing alignment issues, sanding the edges so everything matched, and finishing the wood so it looked polished. This showed me that large projects rarely go exactly according to the first plan, and sometimes the best result comes from being willing to improve the idea as you go.

I also learned a lot about problem-solving and persistence. The biggest challenge was the screen issue. I originally chose smaller I2C screens because they seemed like the easiest option and only required a few pins, but once I started working with them, I realized they were not a good fit for my project. Even though the screens powered on, I could not get them to communicate correctly, which meant I had to step back and rethink my approach. Instead of forcing a bad solution, I ordered different ILI9341 screens, which ended up working much better. That experience taught me that troubleshooting is a major part of any engineering or design process. Not every issue can be solved by pushing harder on the same idea. Sometimes, the better solution is to recognize that a part of the project is not working and make a smarter change before wasting more time.

This project also taught me a lot about myself. I learned that I am able to stay committed to a long project even when it becomes more complicated than I originally expected. I had to come in after school, keep adjusting my design, and continue working through problems instead of giving up when parts of the project became frustrating. I also realized that I care a lot about presentation and detail. I did not just want the scoreboard to function. I wanted it to look good, too, which is why I spent extra time staining the wood, applying polycrylic, and making the stadium design multilayered instead of flat. That showed me that I like projects that combine creativity with technical work, especially when I can build something that is both useful and visually interesting.

If I continued this project, the next step would be improving and expanding it rather than completely redesigning it. I would want to clean up the final construction even more, fully finish the back of the board, and make the electronics enclosure even neater. After that, I would focus on adding more advanced features such as displaying additional game information, improving how the scores refresh, or making the system easier to switch between different games or sports. I would also want to test it during actual NFL games in the fall so I could see it work in the exact setting it was designed for. Overall, this project showed me that large projects take patience, revision, and persistence, but they are also some of the most rewarding because in the end you get to see a real idea become something physical and functional.
